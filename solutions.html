Solutions

These are some of the ways you can slash you carbon emissions

Solar Energy

Shine Bright with Solar

+ More

Solar energy is one of the most widely known forms of renewable energy. It has various uses, and it can be used either residentially or commercially. According to research done by the U.S. Department of Energy, passive solar heating can be integrated into new residential buildings at a small additional price. Along with its fair installation costs, the use of solar energy in homes leads to reduced use of energy sources like oil, electricity, and gas. Although a reduction in the use of these energy sources is beneficial for the environment, it is not necessarily proportional to a reduction in energy costs overall, due to supply and demand. However, a reduction in energy cost is for the most part present, and by choosing solar energy, you aren't just saving some money, but you're also doing your part in the fight against global warming.

Tax Rebate: Up to 26% federal tax credit

Clean/Green Practices: Reduces reliance on fossil fuels, lowers carbon emissions, promotes sustainability.

Wind Energy

Breeze through Clean Energy

+ More

Wind energy is a widely used renewable energy source, mostly for commercial purposes since wind turbines take up a lot of space and require to be set in areas where there is enough wind blowing constantly to generate electricity. However, there are cases where wind energy is used for homes, through the use of smaller scaled wind turbines, designed specifically for homes. These turbines help reduce the reliance on traditional energy sources, lowering residential carbon emissions. Although wind turbines may not completely replace a house's traditional energy usage (oil, gas, electricity), they will ultimately help reduce the monthly energy costs. Wind energy is a reliable source of renewable energy, having your own wind turbine means that you won't be affected by power outages or market fluctuations, making it a smart move for those who want to reduce their environmental footprint, lessen their monthly energy bills, and take control of their own energy use.

Tax Rebate: Up to 30% federal tax credit

Clean/Green Practices: Reduces reliance on fossil fuels, lowers carbon emissions, utilizes natural resources for energy production.

GeoThermal Energy

Stay Cool with Geothermal

+ More

Geothermal energy is another form of renewable energy, mainly used residentially for heating and cooling. There are two main types of a/c residential systems, HVAC and geothermal pumps. Although most houses use HVAC systems, geothermal pumps are found to be more beneficial. Using a geothermal a/c system can cost you less than half of what it would typically cost to have an HVAC installed in your home. Not only is geothermal heating and cooling more cost-effective, it is also generally a more reliable option. Traditional HVAC systems are typically expected to last 10 to 15 years, while geothermal systems usually run for 20 to 25 years. Of the three main residential-friendly renewable energy sources (solar, wind, and geothermal), geothermal energy is the most consistent. This is due to the fact that the Earth's temperature is not as likely to change as much as the weather does on a day-to-day basis.

Tax Rebate: Up to 30% federal tax credit

Clean/Green Practices: Reduces reliance on fossil fuels, lowers carbon emissions, utilizes natural underground heat for energy.
